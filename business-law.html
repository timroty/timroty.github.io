Digital Laws and Their Consequences for Internet Freedom
USA
The United States has a strong legal (and cultural) tradition of upholding citizens' freedom of speech, and the internet is no exception. For this reason, there is little legislation concerning specifically speech online. Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act is the largest piece of legislation regarding this matter, and it dismisses legal liability of providers and content hosts for content created by their users. Without liability for things like defamation or falsehoods, providers must only moderate content when warranted by laws regarding intellectual property, sex trafficking, and electronics communications privacy. Section 230 was intended first to address the central free speech policy vacuum elicited by the internet: is content online to be considered speech of the site, the user, or both? By assigning the speech to the user, the act was intended to foster growth and innovation by absolving internet companies of the burden of content moderation, all while protecting free speech online.
This burden is evidenced in the only recent legislation regarding Section 230: The Allow States and Victims to Fight Online Sex Trafficking Act, or SESTA/FOSTA, signed in April 2018. This law established new liability for internet services when they are involved in the promotion or facilitation of prositution. Even before the bill became law, reports surfaced of the measures companies were taking to avoid fallout from this new liability, such as Craigslist's removal of the "personals" section from its site. Critics of SESTA/FOSTA lamented the incentivization of excessive censorship for companies to avoid liability. An even stronger appeal deemed the act to be unconstitutional based on free speech rights Freedom House.
These two appeals lend themselves to an understanding of freedom of expression on the Internet in the United States beyond just Section 230. The US consistently demonstrates a fierce adherence to their constitutional rights, and this is as evident as anywhere in Reno v. ACLU. In this case, the Supreme Court manifested the nation's constitutional convictions by declaring the Internet to be a free speech zone , warranting at least as much protection under the First Amendment as books, newspapers, and magazines. In his written opinion, Judge Stewart Dalzell designated the Internet as "the most participatory form of mass speech yet developed," one deserving "the highest protection from government intrusion." Thus, there is so little legislation regarding freedom of expression on the Internet because free speech is protected on the Internet as zealous as anywhere, if not even more so.
The US also has a strong aversion to regulation or involvement in private companies' matters, often due to purported infringements on Constitutional rights but also because of the unnecessary burdens and inefficiencies that inevitably arise. Section 230 reflects these values by placing allowing private companies to moderate content as they wish by permitting them to "restrict access to or availability of material that the provider or user considers to be obscene, lewd, lascivious, filthy, excessively violent, harassing, or otherwise objectionable" (Time.com).

Ethical Traditions
USA
In the western philosophical tradition, there are two major systems of thought that dominate any discussion of normative ethics. These are deontology and utilitarianism. The difference between the two is that the former focuses on the morality of actions independent of their consequences, whereas the latter dictates that an action's morality is derived from the consequences of said action. The distinction between the two is highlighted in further detail below.
Deontological ethics derives its roots from scripture and a sense of natural moral law (CRI). This root in religious texts is partially why deontological ethics has no real hold in places like China, where there were no centralized religious institutions to spread a common moral truth (Hastings). More formally, deontology has three important features. The first is that duty is done for duty's sake. The second is that humans should be treated as objects with inherent moral value. The third is that any moral principle must be a categorical imperative that is universalizable, meaning it must apply for everyone in the same moral dilemma (CRI). From these rules we can see clearly that deontology requires an ethical action to be one that is good in and of itself. It must obey some categorical imperative informed by a natural sense of right and wrong. However, this places an enormous weight on an individual’s sense of natural right and wrong, which can vary or even not exist depending on where they are from. Additionally, the third pillar of deontological ethics demanding universalizability can result in a degree of inflexibility that leads to effectively disastrous consequences from a seemingly moral action.
Both deontology and utilitarianism have served as moral justification for various laws and judgements passed in the United States. The notion of natural law and an innate sense of good underpinning deontology is often used as justification for civil liberties in the United States. The connection being that there exist some natural human rights which are innately good that no government can infringe. This then leads to the idea of consent of the governed in the United States (Locke). Utilitarianism, on the other hand, has been used to justify everything from slavery to empire building by western powers (Utilitarianism and Empire). This applies especially to the United States in the mid-nineteenth century when it had its greatest moral struggles emancipating itself from the original sin of its founding and navigating a time of massive expansionism.
China
The legal tradition in China is founded on an ethical basis completely foreign to what many in the West are acquainted with. The ethics that underpin law in China is as varied as the amalgamation of influences that now define the character of the country itself. The Chinese legal code in contemporary use can find traces of its origin in the ancient bureaucracies employed by classical Chinese emperors, various religious teachings, modified german civil law, and even soviet law subsequent to the rise of communism (Mckinney Law). However, throughout the tumult that has defined the evolution of the Chinese legal code, one constant has been the influence of confucianism and legalism (Hastings).
Confucianism and legalism are ideologies that at first glance seem contradictory to one another. Confucianism is rooted in ethical idealism, that humans are inherently good (Britannica). Legalism, on the other hand, sought to introduce strict rule by law that would bring about good by reigning in the excesses of citizens (Britannica). Confucians believe that good conduct and a moral society can be brought about by individuals adhering to li (Hastings). Li are stipulations for moral actions that confucians believe should be inculcated in each individual such that they feel shame when breaking it (Hastings). This shame will then drive them to change their behavior of their own volition. Legalists do not leave the rectification of bad actions to the shame brought about by breaking li. Legalists employ the concept of fa, or strict application of laws, to punish those who break with moral standards (Hastings).
Confucians believe that li can be learned by respecting the five fundamental relationships in any society: ruler to subject, father to son, husband to wife, elder brother to younger brother, and friend to friend (Hastings). These relationships define filial piety, or the prime virtue in confucianism that outlines respect for one's superior. By fulfilling the obligation of filial piety, morality action is guaranteed (Hastings). Legalism finds that order is the root of morality. That an ethical society can only be created through the introduction of order by the strict and egalitarian application of law by the state (Britannica).
With these differences in mind, there is still one great similarity that ties these two systems together - a deep sense of paternalism (hastings). In confucianism, this is conveyed as an individual responsibility to submit those who are your superiors in any relationship (Britannica_Confuciansm). In legalism, this is conveyed as an obligation for the state to restrict the excesses of a citizenry that does not know what is good for themselves (Britannica_Legalism). It is this common strain that informs much of China's lawmaking to this day. More specifically, the confucian-legalist tradition blends these seemingly conflicting philosophies by asking citizens to submit to the state who is a paternal figure (a confucian virtue) so that the state can introduce order and prosperity through strict legislation (a legalist moral imperative).
