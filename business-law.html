Introduction
The following site examines the policies and legislation that guide internet freedoms in the United States and China as well as each culture's ethical traditions that guide the formation of these policies. The United States and China represent starkly different internet systems, with the United States having a largely free and open internet and China having a greatly censored and controlled one. Although there are many other countries with varying degrees of internet freedoms guided by varying ethical traditions, the following analysis focuses on these two countries and their ethical normatives in order to provide an in depth analysis. The final section examines the barriers preventing an international unified internet and its potential future feasibility.
Digital Laws and Their Consequences for Internet Freedom
USA
The United States has a strong legal (and cultural) tradition of upholding citizens' freedom of speech, and the internet is no exception. For this reason, there is little legislation concerning specifically speech online. Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act is the largest piece of legislation regarding this matter, and it dismisses legal liability of providers and content hosts for content created by their users. Without liability for things like defamation or falsehoods, providers must only moderate content when warranted by laws regarding intellectual property, sex trafficking, and electronics communications privacy. Section 230 was intended first to address the central free speech policy vacuum elicited by the internet: is content online to be considered speech of the site, the user, or both? By assigning the speech to the user, the act was intended to foster growth and innovation by absolving internet companies of the burden of content moderation, all while protecting free speech online.
This burden is evidenced in the only recent legislation regarding Section 230: The Allow States and Victims to Fight Online Sex Trafficking Act, or SESTA/FOSTA, signed in April 2018. This law established new liability for internet services when they are involved in the promotion or facilitation of prositution. Even before the bill became law, reports surfaced of the measures companies were taking to avoid fallout from this new liability, such as Craigslist's removal of the "personals" section from its site. Critics of SESTA/FOSTA lamented the incentivization of excessive censorship for companies to avoid liability. An even stronger appeal deemed the act to be unconstitutional based on free speech rights. (Freedom House)
These two appeals lend themselves to an understanding of freedom of expression on the Internet in the United States beyond just Section 230. The US consistently demonstrates a fierce adherence to their constitutional rights, and this is as evident as anywhere in Reno v. ACLU. In this case, the Supreme Court manifested the nation's constitutional convictions by declaring the Internet to be a free speech zone , warranting at least as much protection under the First Amendment as books, newspapers, and magazines. In his written opinion, Judge Stewart Dalzell designated the Internet as "the most participatory form of mass speech yet developed," one deserving "the highest protection from government intrusion." Thus, there is so little legislation regarding freedom of expression on the Internet because free speech is protected on the Internet as zealous as anywhere, if not even more so.
The US also has a strong aversion to regulation or involvement in private companies' matters, often due to purported infringements on Constitutional rights but also because of the unnecessary burdens and inefficiencies that inevitably arise. Section 230 reflects these values by placing allowing private companies to moderate content as they wish by permitting them to "restrict access to or availability of material that the provider or user considers to be obscene, lewd, lascivious, filthy, excessively violent, harassing, or otherwise objectionable" (Time.com).
China
Even as China was ranked as having the least free internet for the sixth consecutive year in 2020, the government continues to tighten their grip on internet access within the country. The most decisive of their censorship tactics is known as the "Great Firewall," a gateway to the global internet that allows the government to restrict connectivity or access to content hosted on servers outside of the country. VPNs allow for circumvention of the Great Firewall, but are essential for businesses operating in China, so they continue to exist with strengthened regulations. The presence of the Great Firewall is nonetheless astounding: 171 of the top 1,000 most globally visited sites and social media platforms around the world are blocked in China. These sites include reputable sources such as the New York Times, the BBC, and most independent Chinese-language news services; media sites such as Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, Instagram, Snapchat, and WhatsApp; and informational resources like Google, Yahoo search, and Wikipedia. In this way, the Great Firewall directly diminishes freedom of expression by limiting not only what Chinese citizens are able to hear, but also what they're able to communicate with others. (Freedom House)

The government has considerable control over freedom of expression within their borders, as well. In addition to the upfront content moderation of the Great Firewall, locally hosted websites and internet companies must proactively monitor their content and remove banned material. Even with the January 2019 arrival of the first foreign telecommunications company in a market dominated by state-owned providers, foreign providers are still forced to control content.
The interplay between these two aspects of control--the upfront gateway between citizens and the global internet, and omnipresent control of the internet within the country--creates a devastatingly effective dynamic for government censorship of freedom of expression. Blocking global search engines has forced almost 90% of market share into the hands of the heavily regulated domestic companies Baidu and Sogou. Foreign news sites are banned in favor of domestic sources, which can be tightly regulated and shut down even without acknowledgement of any specific offense. To force internet providers to tightly enforce censorship regulations, they are subject to suspensions, fines, blacklisting, closure, or even criminal prosecution of relevant personnel. As mentioned, telecomm services are largely state-owned, and still tightly regulated for censorship. In any avenue of the internet, the pattern is clear: the law only allows entities that can be thoroughly regulated, and this statutory presence is used to relentlessly censor content. (Asia Society)
Although authorities claim their practices target pornographic or violent content, independent studies have repeatedly found that content related to politics, religion, economics, and social movements are a primary focus. For example, in order to prevent misinformation and civil unrest before the 30th anniversary of the 1989 Tiananmen Square massacre, the government decided to ban Wikipedia pages of all languages and preemptively censor information regarding the event. Ding Zilin, one of the founders of Tiananmen Mothers, was closely monitored in the weeks leading up to the protests, and her mobile phone service was reportedly cut off. As the 31st anniversary approached, Zoom was compelled to terminate zoom sessions and accounts of organizers intending to commemorate the massacre. This exemplifies how China's pervasive regulatory integration with service and content providers combined with the Great Firewall creates a legal framework in which any information rejected by the government is suffocated by regulators. The ambiguous application of the law has yielded an indiscriminate and extrajudicial state of content moderation, which is accompanied by significant ethical implications beyond merely protests of the past.
Ethical Traditions
USA
In the western philosophical tradition, there are two major systems of thought that dominate any discussion of normative ethics. These are deontology and utilitarianism. The difference between the two is that the former focuses on the morality of actions independent of their consequences, whereas the latter dictates that an action's morality is derived from the consequences of said action. The distinction between the two is highlighted in further detail below.
Deontological ethics derives its roots from scripture and a sense of natural moral law (CRI). This root in religious texts is partially why deontological ethics has no real hold in places like China, where there were no centralized religious institutions to spread a common moral truth (Hastings). More formally, deontology has three important features. The first is that duty is done for duty's sake. The second is that humans should be treated as objects with inherent moral value. The third is that any moral principle must be a categorical imperative that is universalizable, meaning it must apply for everyone in the same moral dilemma (CRI). From these rules we can see clearly that deontology requires an ethical action to be one that is good in and of itself. It must obey some categorical imperative informed by a natural sense of right and wrong. However, this places an enormous weight on an individual’s sense of natural right and wrong, which can vary or even not exist depending on where they are from. Additionally, the third pillar of deontological ethics demanding universalizability can result in a degree of inflexibility that leads to effectively disastrous consequences from a seemingly moral action.

Both deontology and utilitarianism have served as moral justification for various laws and judgements passed in the United States. The notion of natural law and an innate sense of good underpinning deontology is often used as justification for civil liberties in the United States. The connection being that there exist some natural human rights which are innately good that no government can infringe. This then leads to the idea of consent of the governed in the United States (Locke). Utilitarianism, on the other hand, has been used to justify everything from slavery to empire building by western powers (Utilitarianism and Empire). This applies especially to the United States in the mid-nineteenth century when it had its greatest moral struggles emancipating itself from the original sin of its founding and navigating a time of massive expansionism.
China
The legal tradition in China is founded on an ethical basis completely foreign to what many in the West are acquainted with. The ethics that underpin law in China is as varied as the amalgamation of influences that now define the character of the country itself. The Chinese legal code in contemporary use can find traces of its origin in the ancient bureaucracies employed by classical Chinese emperors, various religious teachings, modified german civil law, and even soviet law subsequent to the rise of communism (McKinney Law). However, throughout the tumult that has defined the evolution of the Chinese legal code, one constant has been the influence of confucianism and legalism (Hastings).
Confucianism and legalism are ideologies that at first glance seem contradictory to one another. Confucianism is rooted in ethical idealism, that humans are inherently good (Britannica). Legalism, on the other hand, sought to introduce strict rule by law that would bring about good by reigning in the excesses of citizens (Britannica). Confucians believe that good conduct and a moral society can be brought about by individuals adhering to li (Hastings). Li are stipulations for moral actions that confucians believe should be inculcated in each individual such that they feel shame when breaking it (Hastings). This shame will then drive them to change their behavior of their own volition. Legalists do not leave the rectification of bad actions to the shame brought about by breaking li. Legalists employ the concept of fa, or strict application of laws, to punish those who break with moral standards (Hastings).

Confucians believe that li can be learned by respecting the five fundamental relationships in any society: ruler to subject, father to son, husband to wife, elder brother to younger brother, and friend to friend (Hastings). These relationships define filial piety, or the prime virtue in confucianism that outlines respect for one's superior. By fulfilling the obligation of filial piety, morality action is guaranteed (Hastings). Legalism finds that order is the root of morality. That an ethical society can only be created through the introduction of order by the strict and egalitarian application of law by the state (Britannica).
With these differences in mind, there is still one great similarity that ties these two systems together - a deep sense of paternalism (Hastings). In confucianism, this is conveyed as an individual responsibility to submit those who are your superiors in any relationship (Britannica). In legalism, this is conveyed as an obligation for the state to restrict the excesses of a citizenry that does not know what is good for themselves (Britannica). It is this common strain that informs much of China's lawmaking to this day. More specifically, the confucian-legalist tradition blends these seemingly conflicting philosophies by asking citizens to submit to the state who is a paternal figure (a confucian virtue) so that the state can introduce order and prosperity through strict legislation (a legalist moral imperative).
Thus, in the digital era with its amplification of speech and expression, obeying confucian-legalist morals requires greater state control and surveillance of any online information commons.
Barriers to A Unified Internet
The status quo as it concerns the internet commons is not sustainable. The internet is currently divided into many different spheres with varying degrees of freedom of speech and expression. In the West and especially in the United States, the internet is relatively unregulated with few checks on freedom of speech and expression except for defamation laws. In the East, China and its great firewall stand in stark contrast to this freedom with heavy regulation of digital speech and expression. Both countries believe that they are acting with moral authority when they pursue these policy decisions, however, they do so at the cost of splitting the internet commons in two. Additionally, with two systems, one must be acting in negligence of the moral good if the other is to be acting righteously. Thus, the task is to arrive at some consensus to build a unified internet, where both parties can agree that they are acting morally by participating in it. However, there is one central problem that must be addressed before one can arrive at such a solution.
The issue at the heart of the disagreement between confucian-legalist and western deontological ethical systems is in the existence of some natural law governing human behavior. A central tenet of the confucian-legalist system is that there is no natural law governing human behavior (Hastings). Confucian-legalist tradition dictates that the purpose of li and fa (guiding principles of confucian and legalist ethics respectively), is to introduce law and order where there is none for mankind. Deontology, on the other hand, uses the notion of natural law to justify the intrinsic right or wrong of actions. This difference results in a stark difference from whom moral authority is derived. Since the state is responsible for implementing fa and guiding li as a paternal figure in confucian-legalist teachings, the government becomes the progenitor of moral right and wrong. In the western tradition, however, morality is beyond the purview of the government - they are just another participant in a social contract outlining moral behavior for both the citizenry and the state itself (Locke). This fundamental difference in moral authority needs to be reconciled before any compromise can be made in the level of state control of the internet commons. This is because the suppression of individual freedoms on the internet is regarded as moral or amoral depending on which ethical system is used, and it is not an easy thing to ask either party to submit to the other's moral standard.
A potential solution to this problem is perhaps to just wait. It was only recently in Chinese history that the concept of individual freedoms, at least in the western sense, was legally introduced. It was with Chairman Mao’s death in 1976 that a new China emerged that began protection for individual rights (McKinney Law). Perhaps with more time, the differences between the confucian-legalist tradition and western style ethics will ameliorate itself. Unfortunately, this is likely the best that can be hoped for as the inherent differences between eastern and western ethical systems are too great and too ingrained to be solved swiftly.
